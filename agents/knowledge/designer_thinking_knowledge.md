# Designer Thinking — The Backward Design Framework

A framing for course design that shifts the instructor from *teacher thinking* (what am I covering?) to *designer thinking* (what will students be able to do?).

> If students can't do anything new, it doesn't matter how clear your teaching was.

---

## The Backward Design Framework

Five stages, in order. Each stage is defined by the question it answers, not the artifact it produces.

| Stage | Question |
|---|---|
| **Outcome** | What will students be able to do? |
| **Evidence** | What would convince me they can do it? |
| **Experience** | What practice — with feedback and iteration — gets them there? |
| **Content** | What content serves as a tool toward the outcome? (Content is not the destination.) |
| **Reality Check** | What does success look like in practice, and how could this fail? |

The order matters. Outcome → Evidence comes before Experience and Content. Reality Check is last because it tests the design against use, not against intent.

---

## Teacher vs. Designer

A side-by-side contrast for diagnosing which mode a course (or a single module) is operating in.

| Dimension | Teacher | Designer |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | What am I covering? | What will they do? |
| **Structure** | Topic by topic | Skill by skill |
| **Success** | Got through everything | Students changed |
| **Role** | Content expert | Experience architect |
| **Content** | The destination | The material (a tool) |

When auditing a module, ask: does its structure, success metric, and content treatment match the **Designer** column or the **Teacher** column?

---

## Architects of Learning

Designers attend to four things, in this priority order:

1. **Learners** — who they are, what they bring, what they need to become
2. **Outcomes** — what they will be able to do
3. **Experiences** — the practice and feedback loops that produce capability
4. **Iterations** — designs improve through cycles, not single passes

Content does not appear in this list intentionally. Content is selected in service of the four; it is not one of them.

---

## How to apply this to course audits

When evaluating a course, module, or assignment against this framework:

- **Outcome present?** Is there a clear "students will be able to *X*" statement, or only a topic list?
- **Evidence aligned?** Does the assessment actually require demonstrating the outcome, or only restating content?
- **Experience designed?** Is there practice with feedback before the graded demonstration, or does the first attempt count?
- **Content subordinated?** Is content chosen because it serves the outcome, or because it is "what we cover"?
- **Reality checked?** Has the design been tested against the success scenario *and* a likely failure scenario?

A module that is strong on Content but weak on Outcome/Evidence/Experience is in **teacher mode** even if it looks polished. Flag it.
