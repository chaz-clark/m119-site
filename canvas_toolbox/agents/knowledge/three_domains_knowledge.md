## Why three domains, not one

Hattie tells us *how* learning progresses. The Three Domains tell us *what kinds* of learning a course is designing for. A course can sequence cognitive learning beautifully and still miss the affective and psychomotor objectives its outcomes implicitly require.

| Domain | What it covers | Author |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive** (thinking) | Knowledge, comprehension, reasoning | Bloom (1956); Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) |
| **Affective** (feeling) | Attitudes, values, motivation, social/emotional skills | Krathwohl (1964) |
| **Psychomotor** (kinesthetic) | Physical skill, coordinated movement, embodied practice | Harrow (1972) |

Each taxonomy goes from simple → complex. Wilson is clear:

> The trick in effectively planning lessons — there has to be the **intention for growth** specifically in the selected domain area.

A learning objective only "lives in" a domain if the course **intentionally designs for growth** in that domain. Activity that happens to involve feeling or movement does not count unless the design names it.

## The Cognitive Domain

The thinking taxonomy. Used to specify what level of cognitive work a learning objective requires.

### Bloom 1956 vs. Anderson/Krathwohl 2001

The 2001 revision changed two things: **noun → verb** and **swapped the top two levels** (creating now sits above evaluating).

| # | Bloom 1956 | Anderson/Krathwohl 2001 |
|---|-----------|-------------------------|
| 1 | Knowledge | Remembering |
| 2 | Comprehension | Understanding |
| 3 | Application | Applying |
| 4 | Analysis | Analyzing |
| 5 | Synthesis | **Evaluating** ← swapped |
| 6 | Evaluation | **Creating** ← swapped (now highest) |

The newer version is preferred for new instructional planning.

> Full taxonomy with verb lists: see [`blooms_taxonomy_knowledge.md`](blooms_taxonomy_knowledge.md) (forthcoming, built from the Taxonomy Explorer source).

## The Affective Domain

Five levels of internalization. Krathwohl (1964).

| # | Level | What it means | Sample verbs |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Receiving** | Awareness; willingness to attend | feel, sense, capture, experience, attend, perceive |
| 2 | **Responding** | Active attention, motivation to engage | conform, allow, cooperate, contribute, enjoy, satisfy |
| 3 | **Valuing** | Acceptance, preference, or commitment to a value | believe, seek, justify, respect, persuade |
| 4 | **Organization** | Sorting values into a coherent system | examine, clarify, systematize, integrate |
| 5 | **Characterization** | Values become a stable life pattern | internalize, resolve, judge |

### Why the affective domain matters for any course

Wilson's argument applies even to "non-emotional" subjects (math, sciences, IT):

> Since **emotion draws both attention and channels strong residual memory**, it behooves all dedicated and artful educators to include affective objectives, no matter what their discipline.

Affective objectives raise the **memorability** of cognitive content — directly relevant to long-term retention (see CLT schema formation).

### Where affective growth shows up in any course

- Group work or cooperative exercises (deportment, collaboration)
- Independent intellectual risk-taking (developing and presenting a hypothesis)
- Persuading peers on conclusions
- Engaging with controversial or value-laden topics
- Self-confidence development through challenge

If any outcome implies these, the course should **name** an affective objective for them — not let them happen incidentally.

## The Psychomotor Domain

Six levels of physical/kinesthetic skill. Harrow (1972).

| # | Level | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Reflex movements** | Involuntary; present at birth or via maturation |
| 2 | **Fundamental movements** | Walking, running, pushing, pulling, manipulating |
| 3 | **Perceptual abilities** | Kinesthetic, visual, auditory, tactile coordination |
| 4 | **Physical abilities** | Endurance, flexibility, agility, strength, dexterity |
| 5 | **Skilled movements** | Skills for games, sports, dances, performances, arts |
| 6 | **Nondiscursive communication** | Expressive movement (posture, gesture, mime, ballet) |

### Critical boundary — physical ≠ psychomotor

This boundary determines whether a hands-on activity belongs in the psychomotor domain at all:

> If you are using a physical activity to **support** a cognitive or affective function, label it physical/kinesthetic — **not psychomotor**. Psychomotor means there is a very clear educational intention for growth in the psychomotor/kinesthetic domain itself.

**Examples:**

| Activity | Domain |
|---|---|
| Looking through a microscope to identify cells | Cognitive (physical-supporting-cognitive) |
| Coding lab where students implement a SQL pipeline | Cognitive (physical-supporting-cognitive) |
| Practicing surgical suture technique for proficiency | Psychomotor (intent is the physical skill) |
| Learning dance steps for performance | Psychomotor |

For most BYUI courses (especially in IT/Data domains), **psychomotor objectives are rare**. Most "hands-on" activity is physical-supporting-cognitive and should be tagged as cognitive in the audit.

### Embodied learning (relevant exception)

> Targeted physical movement has the potential to enhance memory and recall and can aid in accelerating long-term memory. Intentionally adding movement to enhance learning is often called *embodied learning*.

Even when the primary domain is cognitive, intentionally including movement (gestures while reasoning, walking while reflecting, building artifacts with hands) can strengthen schema formation. Worth flagging when present and absent.

## Holistic design — all three domains

Wilson's design recommendation:

> Wise teachers combine domains so that lessons and learning are more **holistic and multidimensional**. Mixing domains and using more diversity in delivering lessons also helps students create more neural networks and pathways thus aiding their retention and recall.

A course that addresses only the cognitive domain leaves retention strength on the table.

## Canvas Audit Indicators

### Cognitive domain present?

Almost always yes for any university course. Audit question is *what level* — see Bloom file. Quick check: do learning outcomes use higher-order verbs (analyze, evaluate, create) or stop at remember/understand?

### Affective domain present?

| Signal | What it indicates |
|---|---|
| Outcomes mention collaboration, ethics, judgment, professional behavior | Affective objective is implied; check if it's named |
| Discussions use prompts that require students to take a position, persuade, or risk being wrong | Affective design intent (Valuing or higher) |
| No discussion/peer activity in any module | Affective domain absent — flag as gap |
| Group projects with no rubric criteria for collaboration itself (only for the deliverable) | Affective happening incidentally, not designed |
| Reflection prompts asking students to evaluate their own engagement, growth, or beliefs | Characterization-level affective design |

### Psychomotor domain present?

| Signal | What it indicates |
|---|---|
| Course outcomes name a physical skill (lab technique, performance, build) as the goal | Psychomotor objective intentional — fully audit |
| "Hands-on" activity but the assessed outcome is conceptual | Physical-supporting-cognitive — tag as cognitive, not psychomotor |
| Course is fully online with no required physical artifact | Psychomotor likely absent and that's fine |
| Movement/embodiment intentionally used to aid cognitive learning | Embodied learning — note as cognitive enhancement |

## Domain Tags Used in Audit Output

Add a `learning_domain` field to each audit issue:

| Value | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| `cognitive` | Issue affects thinking/knowledge objectives |
| `affective` | Issue affects feeling/value/motivation objectives |
| `psychomotor` | Issue affects intentional physical-skill objectives |
| `multi` | Issue spans more than one domain |

Pair with `hattie_phase` and `cognitive_load_type` for full diagnosis: *which domain* (Three Domains) is affected at *which phase* (Hattie) due to *what kind of load* (CLT).

## Quick Reference for Auditors

When evaluating a course, module, or assignment, ask:

1. **Cognitive level** — what Bloom level do the outcomes target? Are activities/assessments at the same level?
2. **Affective intent** — do any outcomes imply collaboration, judgment, persuasion, or values? If yes, are affective objectives **named** or just hoped for?
3. **Psychomotor intent** — does the course require an intentional physical skill as its goal? (Most non-lab/non-performance courses: no.) If activity is physical but the outcome is conceptual, tag as cognitive.
4. **Embodiment** — could intentional movement strengthen retention here? Note as enhancement opportunity, not a gap.
5. **Holistic check** — does the course span more than one domain, or only cognitive? Flag single-domain courses as a retention risk per Wilson.

A course that scores well on cognitive but ignores affective is a common pattern in IT/sciences courses. The audit should surface this so the instructor can decide whether to design intentional affective objectives.
